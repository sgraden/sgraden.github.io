IBM Cloud Virtual Private Cloud (Under Construction)

The Virtual Private Cloud (VPC) project is the next evolution for the infrastructure offerings of IBM Cloud. These upgrades made it easier for our clients to grow and evolve their applications.

Role: UX Design / Lead
Timeline: August 2017 - June 2019

Intro

The VPC project story is long, complex, and a good learning opportunity for me. Out of necessity, I am going to avoid telling it chronologically and highlight events, opportunities, and challenges from the journey.

Prior to the release of VPC, IBM Cloud Infrastructure was offering the same services as their acquisition, Softlayer, had been providing for years. The Softlayer suite of offerings were very similar management experiences to having a physical datacenter. Relative to competition, it just wasn’t as flexible, simple, or private.

VPC offerings were upgrades on the hardware and software of the Softlayer infrastructure. These upgrades made it easier for our clients to grow and evolve their applications while maintaining a more isolated environment.

Brief Summary

The VPC project, codenamed Genesis, was one of the longest projects I have been on. While this project was one of innovation, exploration, and evolution of our offerings it was also filled with the complexities of communication, priorities, hopes and fears.

Genesis was born from the need to evolve the flexibility and simplicity of our powerful physical technology to keep up with the expectations of the cloud market. At the same time, our Innovation Lab was leading this charge with an advanced new piece of network hardware to leap-frog our competition. At this point the design was being solely driven by my future design lead Megan Baxter.

While Megan had been working on VPC for around 6 months, I was working on our Networking Design Team. My primary focus was our new offerings CDN and Cloud Internet Services (See project). Our network team began getting notice of a new project codenamed “Wrigley” (after Wrigley Field, see IBM can be fun!).

Wrigley was a new networking tech that was created for evolving the flexibility and simplicity of our powerful, physical technology...wait this sounds familiar.

As we were being on-boarded to this project, our design team immediately began noticing that this was doing exactly what the Genesis project was, albeit with a shorter time frame and with the goal of catching up to competition Vs. leap-frogging. Along with our design work, we began prioritizing improving communication between teams to help determine the future of the projects and our go-to-market strategies.

Work on both projects moved in parallel while additional discussions were being had. As designers working in close proximity, Megan, her team (which was now getting involved), and I made sure to operate with the same design paradigms in preparation of the eventual merging of the products. Or at least that is what we hoped in order to have a user experience that we would consider positive.

My product areas

Virtual Private Cloud - The core networking component required for creating any application. A VPC is an isolated virtual network that creates a buffer to keep all of your other services away from public networks and other clients on the IBM network. However, it still maintains the flexibility to customize the network exactly how your application needs.

Subnets - A smaller subsection of your VPC. This network designates a range of IP addresses other services can use to communicate. Individual subnets operate as communication zones for everything in the subnet, but a client can place specific security requirements when communicating in and out of the subnet.

Public Gateway - A service placed on a subnet to allow services on that subnet to initiate communication to the public internet through this gateway using Network Address Translation.

Floating IP - A publicly addressable IP address placed on a Virtual Server’s Network Interface Card. Use this to make an application, like a website, reachable.

Services I worked on

During the 1.5-2 year VPC project, we had 3 consistent sources of user feedback. The first, and most available, were our internal beta testers. We would then turn to usertesting.com for external validation and catching simple oversights. Finally, our more critical, but slightly harder to access, source was our whitelisted, early access external clients.

When it comes to research, something fun about having legitimate reasons to talk to internal users, is that they will not hold back. When we had questions, assumptions, or designs, we would take them to an internal Slack channel specific to this user group. Because it was internal (and free), we often took less refined, more explorative design ideas there to have quick iterations and gut checks.

Our internal teams tended to represent the more extreme side of an enterprise development team. They enjoyed GUI, but truly focused on having a powerful CLI and API. One of our most impactful discoveries from this group was the importance of offering the CLI/API command for an action, directly in the GUI. These technical users were going through a process of learning when they were in the GUI and seeing how clickable inputs affected a CLI/API command allowed them to learn how to use it, as well as immediately take their work from the GUI and make it scale productively.

Moments of delight: IP Range Visualization

At the core of an applications infrastructure is its network which is broken into a series of groups of IP addresses called “Subnets”. Subnets are generally notated in an “IP Range” or “CIDR” format such as 192.168.0.0/24. That converts to the range 192.168.0.1 to 192.168.0.254 for a total of 256 IP addresses that can be connected to 256 network devices such as a computer. The other trick to this is that IP ranges generally aren’t allowed to overlap with each other. While simple arrangements of networks are relatively easy to navigate, more complex situations become exhausting and unpleasant to work with. This led a number of our users to create various other documents just to track their environment.

Through a brainstorming session with our development team, designers, and offering managers, we noticed that while many network architects have grown to accept this process, we could feasibly make it simpler for those that prefer.

This initiative was largely guided by our VPC design lead, Megan Baxter. She arranged a series of session where our team of 4 designers diverged and converged, identified focuses, iterated, got feedback from development teams, and ultimately built an IP Range visualization and selector.

While I was a participant in the designs, I tried to also be the touchpoint for the math and technology behind these ranges. Most of my knowledge came from reviewing videos, reading articles, and studying portions of “AWS Certified Solutions Architect” course on Udemy.com. I then organized the important information into a quick crash course for our team. This helped our team to have a more targeted and realistic approach to the problem and to help with motivation and moral around the problem (Working on something you don't understand is a fast way to get someone to stop working on it).

Where competitors simply offer a textbox to enter in an IP range that does not conflict with other subnets, we went a step further. First off, when the user begins creating a network, we prefill a non-conflicting default IP range that fills the next logical “block” of the network. Without thinking, the user can continue on. However, for those who need to customize it, they can begin manipulating the network size and range with a few buttons and see, with a visualization, how it fits amongst other subnetworks they have. Of course, for those who prefer manually entering a value, they can still do that, but with that added bonus of the visualization rather than an error that simply says “conflicts with other networks”.
